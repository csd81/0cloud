Preface
Cloud technology is a relatively new but exciting area of computing. Several decades of research and
development effort of academia and industry into distributed systems, networking, virtualisation,
service-oriented computing turned into a practical reality providing virtually limitless computing and
storage resources on which new type of applications and services can be provided to millions of users.
The purpose of this book is to provide an introduction to the programming aspects of cloud systems.
As cloud systems are increasingly operated by highly-specialised data centres of large tech companies,
it is the software were the real opportunities lie for the individual developers and small companies.
The amount of material that we could possibly cover in this book is enormous; therefore, the focus is
only on introducing the most fundamental concepts, methods and programming techniques to
students, which should prepare them for further, more specialised study of selected specific areas.
The content of this book is strongly related to the structure and content of the Cloud Programming
course offered to MSc students by the Faculty of Information Technology at the University of Pannonia
in Veszprem, Hungary. While the book is meant to be introductory, working knowledge of Java
programming, fundamental concepts of computer architecture, operating systems, distributed
systems is a pre-requisite for this course. The chapters do not necessarily need to be read in the order
they are presented in the book, but for readers not familiar with the topic, this might be the most
suitable sequence.
While every care has been taken to create a correct manuscript, errors and omissions might be
present. Any of these is the sole responsibility of the author. In hope of further improving this book,
readers are welcome to send their feedback and comments to the author.

1 Introduction
Over the past decade, the “cloud” has become a household name. We store our data in the cloud, use
cloud services such as Gmail, Amazon, Netflix, Spotify, various web shops, online news services, etc.
every day without thinking about where they are exactly in the physical sense, how they are
implemented and operated. We take their existence and availability for granted.
It took the computing and IT industry several decades of research and development, thousands of
person-years of effort to create these systems, and above all, the cloud itself. We all know the
buzzwords about the cloud: unlimited storage and computation, on-demand use, low cost, scalability,
high-availability, elasticity or scaling, integration of services, and the like. We also know that
somewhere in the world, there are large datacentres that are the backbone of the cloud
infrastructure. These are mainly operated by large global corporations, due to the extremely high level
of initial investment required to become cloud providers, as well as to be able to provide services at a
truly global scale.
This book is about the cloud through the eye of a software developer. We will not be interested in the
operational or daily management aspects of the cloud. What we will focus on is how this new
‘medium’ affects the software development practice, the way we create and deploy applications.
From the following common cloud-related terms: Cloud Computing, Cloud Architecture, Cloud
Programming, we will be looking at the last one, Cloud Computing in detail.
The aim of this book – and the course – is not just simply to provide practical programming tips; I hope
that by the end of the course, students will have a perspective, a deeper understanding of the
theoretical and technological drives that pushed our industry towards the Cloud. To appreciate the
main challenges of modern computing and software development, Chapter 2 will provide a brief
overview (or recap) of key developments that can be viewed as precursors or pre-requisites to the
birth of Cloud Technology. We will touch topics such as resource sharing, developments in networking,
various high-performance computing systems, the programming challenges of distributed systems,
and so on. The maturity of these fields provided the foundations on which the Cloud could be built.
The next part of the book will look at the Cloud in a generic manner. Their key characteristics,
architectural, operational and software-related aspects will be discussed. This will be followed by an
overview of the key cloud providers, their short history, market share and relevance, as well as a brief
list of their service offerings.
Then we turn to the programming aspects. We will start with the infrastructure components: storage,
computation and data transfer (networking), as these are the core building blocks of any cloud system.
This will be followed by higher-level services that make developers’ life easier. Examples to these are
application services, specific application platforms that are typically used in web-based system
development.
Building on the concept of scalability, we will explore how to execute large computational tasks by
using multiple execution services. Popular execution patterns, such as Map/Reduce will be explored.
For the execution examples, but throughout the book, in general, practical examples will be given
based on the Google Cloud Platform (GCP). The reason for this is the generous support of Google for
several years in providing free access for our students to their cloud system, and the level of support
and documentation available for the interested students. In the final chapters of the book, streaming
data processing, Cloud Function, Publish/Subscribe communication, AI services and service
orchestration will be discussed.

2 Technological developments of the past: the road to Cloud
Computing
The Holy Grail of computing is a system that has exceptionally high performance, it is free or very
inexpensive to use, accessible by anyone, reliable and pervasive, it provides fast, effective and simple
ways for application development and deployment, and at the same time provides maximum user
experience and usability for end users. We have not found this magic system yet but the Cloud is
probably the one among the existing candidates that is the closest to it.
The goal of this chapter is to introduce the problems typically found in large computing systems and
overview various technological solutions invented in the past decades for solving them. It is also our
aim to illustrate that Cloud Computing is not entirely new, unique concept, but rather the result of
decades of systematic research and development in distributed computing systems.
The overview of technological developments in this chapter will focus no so much on the technical
details of each particular technology (we assume the readers are already familiar with these
fundamental topics) but on the problems they tried to solve, the way they did it, and to draw up a
conceptual path (by “joining the dots”) that leads to the emergence of cloud computing. At the end
of each subsection, we will emphasise how a given piece of technology or its fundamental concepts
find their place in modern cloud computing systems.

2.1 Access to and sharing computational resources
The first central problem area that we examine is what we call the problem of resource sharing. A
computing system should be owned and operated in a way that it minimises its cost, maximises its
utilisation and the performance observed by the users. Unfortunately, these are conflicting needs that
are hard to satisfy simultaneously. Let us look back to the history of computing and see in what form
and level the issue of resource sharing appeared in various computing systems.

2.1.1 Limited resource systems – mainframes
In the early days of computing, computers were large, expensive and scarce, typically located in
specific computing facilities. These mainframe computers (see Figure 2-1) required specially trained
technical personnel for operating and programming the machines. The computers executed a limited
number of special-purpose programs dedicated to particular research or business problems. Most
systems could only execute one program at a time, so execution time slots had to be reserved in
advance. Since the computers were centrally hosted and managed, the system administrators were
able to create optimal program execution schedules that kept the utilisation rate of the computer high
(consequently, its effective cost low). Note that the unit operating cost of computer system depends
on its utilisation rate. If a system whose initial cost is, say, 10 000 USD operates 100 000 hours (approx.
11 years) at 100% utilisation, the cost of 1 hour program execution is 0.1 dollar. If the same system is
idle 90% of the time, the effective program execution cost increases to 1 USD/h. Therefore, keeping
utilisation rate high is an important goal in system operation.
Later technological improvements resulted in time-shared or time-sliced operating modes, in which
users could use the computer at the same time via terminals. Despite the continuous improvements,
these centralised mainframe systems had several disadvantages for end users:
-

The shared nature of the computer meant that users had to wait for one another; programs could
not be executed before the previous running instance had finished.

In time-sliced multiplexed mode, each user program executed at a reduced speed. The
performance decrease was proportional to the number of simultaneously connected users.
The central administration resulted in strict rules to follow, which affected how and when the
system could be accessed and what programs could be executed.

2.1.2 Personal computers, Networking and Client-Server systems
The Personal Computer (PC) revolution starting in the 1980s brought a radical shift in attitude to
shared computer systems. As a result of miniaturisation (mass manufacturing of integrated circuits),
the cost of computers dropped to the level that allowed first small businesses and later individuals to
own a computer. One of the main argument in favour of the PC was that users no longer had to share
their computer with others; they could use it whenever they want and for whatever they wish. The
fact that users had to train themselves to be able to operate, administer and manage the computers
was not advertised loudly…
With the computers came the need for peripherals, too. Users started to buy small printers, tape
backup devices, and so on. Typically, these devices were used relatively infrequently. In a business
environment, the additional cost of employees using private printers was coupled with the problems
of data sharing. Individual PCs stored their data on their local hard drive. People working on different
stages of a larger project had to copy and carry around files required by other colleagues as input. Not
much changed as technology improved, data transfer moved on from floppy disks to CDs.

After a surge in non-shared computers, most institutes realised the need for sharing, both for financial

implemented functionality. Common examples of server types include FTP, email, time, database and

2.1.3 Supercomputers
Although personal computers certainly destroyed the mainframe computer industry, it remained and
fact that not every computing task could be solved with personal computers. For problems that were
exceptionally demanding in a computational sense, special, much more powerful computers were
created. These supercomputers took over the role of the former mainframe computers.
Supercomputers, per definition, are the most powerful computers of the world at a given time. They
are very expensive, consequently located at centralised supercomputer centres. Large countries have
several supercomputer centres, small ones might have one or none at all. Access to supercomputers
is limited, only a selected and approved set of users can run programs on them. Program execution is

programs (called jobs) are executed when all required resources (processors, memory, storage) are
available for the task. Consequently, supercomputer systems employ queuing systems for job
execution. Users submit their jobs to one of several execution queues and wait for the program to run
and produce results. Note the similarity to early mainframe systems where users had to reserve
computer time to run their programs. Sharing supercomputers is a necessity in order to achieve high
utilisation rate that will keep operating costs at the minimum.
The performance of supercomputers is measured by the number of floating points operations (flop)
they can execute in one second (flop/s). Performance is normally measured in multiples of thousand
flops, such as Mflop/s (106), Glop/s (109), Tflop/s (1012), Pflop/s (1015), etc. The first computer that was
considered as a supercomputer was the CDC 6600 manufactured by Control data Corporation,

2.2.1 Remote Procedure Call
It should come as no surprise that this problem was discovered very early by the distributed systems
programming community and thus standardisation work began. The first proposal that became
influential was the Remote Procedure Call (RPC) by Sun Microsystems [5]. The key philosophy of RPC
is to present a programming interface for accessing and using a remote server that developers are
already familiar with. This natural common abstraction is the procedure/function call well known for
every programmer. If we can hide the details and complexities of connection setup, message
packaging and transfer, programmers can focus more on program logic and simultaneously, the
quality of the programs will increase. Such a standardised system can only work if there is a special
“runtime” that performs these behind-the-scene tasks on behalf of the programmer. In distributed
systems terminology, this “runtime” is called the middleware, a software layer sitting between the
distributed application and the operating system. The role of the middleware layer is to create a
common layer of abstraction known to all collaborating parties in the distributed system.
To access this middleware layer (the RPC runtime) we need to create special code sections, called stub,
that connect the client and server code with the middleware [6]. Figure 2-10 illustrates the operation
of the RPC call via the stubs. The stubs are generated by an RPC compiler.

2.2.2 Remote Method Invocation
Java occupies a special position in distributed systems due to its platform-independence. The Java
Virtual Machine provides an identical runtime environment to Java applications regardless of what
hardware and operating system is used on a given computer. Since Java supports object persistence
(serialisation), it is possible to send not only data but Java classes from one VM to another. This is the
basis of the Java version (or re-implementation) of RPC, the Remote Method Invocation (RMI)
technology [7].
The goal of RMI is to provide a remote method calling mechanism to Java programs. Since a Java
program runs in the JVM and is isolated from the hardware, the standard Java method call mechanism
is not suitable for accessing remote computers. The designers of RMI introduced a middleware layer
that cooperates with the underlying operating system to build connections and transfer data to a
remote party. In addition, since Java is a 100% object-oriented language, a special abstraction – a stub
class – is introduced that represents the remote server to the client as a normal Java object during
operation.
There is a bit of additional infrastructure required to get RMI working. Since the object persistence
mechanism only transfers object state, an additional way must be provided to ship the compiled Java

interface that captures the functionality of the server. This means that clients must know the interface
they want to use, as this interface will provide the public methods that a client can invoke. The use of
interfaces provides an improved description of remote functionality than possible with port numbers.
Now, a remote system can have arbitrary number of functionalities available to the clients, as long as

2.2.3 The case for loosely coupled systems
The RPC/RMI programming abstraction is a great help in reducing complexity and program errors in
distributed software development. It provides a natural extension of the well-known
function/procedure/method call technique. It turns out, however, that it is a bit fragile in the presence
of errors. Hiding the details of low-level communication, as well as ‘pretending’ the server is just
another ordinary function of an object imply to many programmers that the system they work with
behaves exactly the same way as a traditional locally executing program. Nothing could be further
from the truth! Distributed servers can manifest various errors that are non-existing in sequential
programs. The network connection can break down, the server can have a transient or fatal error,
producing incorrect or no results. These situations are difficult to monitor and control with function
calls. RMI uses the special Java exceptions (RemoteException) that signal the caller if something went
wrong.
The real difficulty comes in long method call chains; when several distributed system components call
each other to execute a complex task. Let’s see an example in which a client C will call server A. Server
A, in turn will call server B to help, which then will call server D. The client might be unaware of this
implementation detail, as it only connected to server A. When no errors occur, the system works
perfectly, but if an error happens, the state of the entire system can easily become corrupt. Think of
a task that requires transactional integrity. How can you guarantee that a task executes only if all
subtasks run correctly? If any member of the call chain generates an error, a domino effect starts and
14

exceptions (or error codes) will ripple through the system in a backward direction. In addition, if one
method call hangs indefinitely (due to waiting or a deadlock situation), each caller method will be
blocked as well. This mode of operation is called strong coupling in distributed systems. In other
words, an error in one component directly affects the operation of another component. The complete
failure of one server will block the entire system, and the tasks are no longer executable. As shown in
Figure 2-12, there are many possible error locations in a distributed method call chain. It is rather
difficult to locate the exact error cause from a single returned RemoteException instance; moreover,
it is even more difficult to guarantee the state integrity of the system in such a situation.

inherent to the strongly coupled method chaining abstraction became apparent. The industry decided
to turn to loosely coupled communicating systems, relying on message-passing type communication
and asynchronous execution. We will look these in more detail later.

2.3 Service oriented systems
The concept of service orientation grew out of client-server systems and the use of interfaces for
describing server functionality. In order to make distributed systems more flexible, less tied to
hardware, researchers were looking at ways to decouple the system executing the server code from
the functionality the server represents. The concept of the service appeared as a suitable
generalisation. A service is a piece of functionality implemented in hardware or software that provides
a useful function for the client. A service is represented by a contract between the service provider
and service customer. The key novelty in service-oriented software systems is the ability to find service
providers based on their service instead of the server address that makes it physically executable. For
instance, if I want to translate a document from, say, German to English, I am only interested in online
services that can provide me with this translation. I am not concerned where they run (IP address), I
only care that the translation will be done. How can this be achieved? We will see in the rest of this
section.

15

Service-oriented systems are based on the following fundamental principles: (i) the service is
described by an interface; (ii) there is a directory that mediates between clients and services (services
register and clients look up services in the directory); (iii) Clients and services interact only for the
duration of the service execution; (iv) clients and services use open, standardised technologies and
protocols.

Figure 2-13 The generic architecture and operation of a service-oriented system.
Service-orientation started to become popular from the year 2000. As with any heterogeneous
distributed system, the first key problem was to establish a platform-neutral communication and
protocol framework. After this, the design and implementation of service-oriented systems could
began. The promise of service-oriented computing was to create Internet-enabled “components” with
well-defined functionality from which larger, applications that are more complex could be created by
relatively straightforward integration processes.
The first candidate for middleware technology was XML. Interestingly, this is not a programming
language but a mark-up language. XML, however, was always meant to be used by computers, not
humans, carries a strict, rule-based approach to describing data and operations. The idea was to use
XML to describe service functionality, plus use it in data communication and service invocations, too.
For the latter, additional tools had to be developed that generated “stubs” for target programming
languages from the service interfaces. Not surprisingly, the first XML-based service systems used an
RPC-style service invocation.

2.3.1 XML based services – web services
When XML and service-orientation is used on top of the HTTP protocol, we talk about Web Service
technology. Web services are services designed for invocation via the Internet using Web protocols.
They were meant to create the backbone for new generation of business-to-business applications that
will simplify performing business online and creating systems that can be easily maintained and
modified in the future. Figure 2-14 illustrates the general architecture of a Web Service based serviceoriented system.
Services are described by an XML-based WSDL (Web Service Description Language) interface
descriptor that carries information about the location of the service, the functionality it offers and the
way it can be accessed. This description is stored in a public directory, called UDDI (Universal
Description, Discovery, and Integration). The directory is also XML-based. Clients use the UDDI
directory to find suitable services, retrieve the necessary information for accessing the service, then

16

using XML-based SOAP (Simple Object Access Protocol) messages, connect to the service and execute
chosen functions.

Figure 2-14 The Web Service architecture
Similar to web servers, the recommendation for web services is to implement stateless services.
Stateless services are not dependent on the order of invocations, they do not alter their own state
and always perform the same operation to any incoming request, which make them tolerant to many
types of errors found in large distributed systems. Unfortunately, many services, especially clientdependent business services are stateful, which led to the support of state in services. The increasing
complexities necessitated the extension of the simple WSDL-SOAP based client-service interaction
technology. Several new layers and components appeared in the Web Service technology stack (as
illustrated in Figure 2-15) that were responsible for various additional operational functionality (e.g.
managing distributed transactions, providing context, orchestrating services, describing business
workflows, etc.). This, unfortunately resulted in such a complex and large technology system that is
almost impossible to understand. The set of Web Service specifications, colloquially called the "WS-*
stack" contains over 70 specifications.

2.3.2 RESTful services
Representational State Transfer (REST for short) is the result of Roy Fielding’s PhD work in 2000 and is
the basis of an alternative approach to creating large distributed service systems. Unhappy with the
complexity of mainstream service-oriented approaches, Fielding suggested to use a simple, resourceoriented way to describe services. Whereas traditional approaches were centred around the notion
of computation (executing something on behalf of the client), REST looked at the problem from the
data point of view. Fielding suggested that everything (even a single byte in memory) could have a
URL through which its state can be queried and modified. For these, only a GET and POST HTTP
commands are required along with the URL pointing to the resource. He developed a method to
describing service interfaces using URLs including GET and POST commands.
The simplicity of REST-based or RESTful services made this approach popular very quickly, creating a
real clash of opinions within the Web Service community. Nonetheless, REST seems to be the clear
winner by now, leaving XML based Web Services used for new systems development only in
exceptional circumstances. Details of REST can be found in Fielding’s dissertation7 or at the following
Wikipedia entry: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representational_state_transfer.

2.4 Grid computing
Grid Computing emerged at the end of the 1990s as the result of two independent, simultaneous
movements. Supercomputers in the US operated in an isolated fashion which resulted in large regional
differences in supercomputer loads. One supercomputer centre could become overbooked resulting
in user jobs waiting in work queues while some other centre at the same time were underutilised not
receiving enough jobs to keep the system busy. Researchers proposed that by connecting these
centres into a larger system which would automatically send jobs to the most suitable centre could
increase the overall efficiency and utilisation of supercomputer centres and would reduce waiting time
for users. About the same time, networking and distributed computing systems reached the level of
maturity that allowed the construction of large compute clusters from local area connected
computers. Researchers proposed to extend this concept to wide-area networks, i.e. connect distant
computers together to create large virtual parallel computers. The first effort was proposing
connecting supercomputers, while the second was planning the same with clusters of computers. Both
were seeking to increase the efficiency and utilisation of existing compute resources by providing an
efficient load-balancing software layer.
The term Grid Computing was introduced to compare access to computing to access to electricity. In
the US, the electrical power distribution system is called the Power Grid. The idea was to provide
computing and storage facilities for researcher regardless where they are geographically. If a
computer is plugged into the Grid, it can access resources, just as we get electricity when we plug our
device into the mains socket. Early efforts resulted in various software systems aiming at providing a
high-level programming interface for creating and running parallel programs on distributed clusters.
Notable systems include the Parallel Virtual Machine (PVM)[8], Legion [9], and the Globus Toolkit [10].
These early systems were initially focusing on creating small cluster based systems looking at ways to
efficiently use idle computers in university clusters but, as experience accumulated, larger and larger
wide-area systems were attempted. By 2005, nearly all developed countries were creating national
grid systems. In Europe, the DataGrid project [10] led by CERN (with the participation of several

The most successful middleware software development project was the Globus toolkit that

FTP, etc) instead of creating new programming abstractions (such as PVM or Legion). We will use an

Let us assume that a researcher wants to run a simulation program. This particular program is a

with representative members of academic and business communities.

2.5 Voluntary resource sharing
An alternative of the push-style Grid systems, in which users send their program for execution to
remote sites, is voluntary computing; in which computer owners donate their computers for program
execution. Programs are deployed in a pull-style by connecting a central task database.
The first and most well-known example for such a computational system is the SETI@home project.
The project started in 1999 at the University of Berkeley with the idea to use idle computer resources
to find signs of extra-terrestrial life by analysing radio telescope data. In this system8, users installed a
program that would run as a screensaver but instead of drawing random graphics on screen, it would
connect to the SETI server, download a segment of telescope data and analyse it. The simplified
architecture of the system is shown in Figure 2-18. It is interesting to note that in this system the client

is fully automatic and the operation is fault-tolerant; the operation is continuous even in the presence

2.6 Hosting and the emerging utility computing model
By the late 1990s, early 2000s computer have become indispensable for the daily operation of

2.7 Virtualisation
Virtualisation as a technology has been known for a long time and was used routinely in mainframe
computers. The goal of virtualisation is to abstract away or disconnect a software system from its
hardware environment by creating a virtual environment, most of the time in the form of a virtual
machine. Virtualisation has gained attention and popularity with the emergence of hosting providers
and data centres.
In the past, a software could only run on a hardware onto which it was compiled. This resulted in a
brittle infrastructure and vendor “lock-in”. For instance, if a computer became obsolete, it became
more and more difficult to maintain the required hardware infrastructure and keep the software
operational. Also, developing a system for a particular operating system became a “life-time” choice,
as it was impossible to move to a different platform once the application was developed.
The use of virtual machines enables the creation of a more flexible and efficient computing
infrastructure. With virtualisation, it is now possible to virtualise a hardware resource and run any
operating system on top of it (Figure 2-20). Similarly, it is possible to virtualise an operating system
and run applications on it although the underlying platform is running an incompatible operating
system.
As shown in Figure 2-21, there are various areas of virtualisation (hardware, network, storage,
memory and so on). In hardware virtualization, a virtualisation layer called Virtual Machine Monitor
(VMM) is used that is responsible for emulating the hardware to the operating system. The goal of
hardware virtualisation is to be able to run complete operating systems on any hardware without
22

modification. The virtualisation layer will emulate an instruction set architecture and hardware view
that is required by the guest operating system.

Figure 2-20 Virtualisation allows running different operating systems on the same hardware
In case of Full Virtualization, the complete simulation of the actual hardware takes place to allow
software to run an unmodified guest OS.
Paravirtualization represents an interesting approach to hardware virtualisation. In this type of
virtualization, the virtualisation layer present a special API to the operating system that allows some
functionality of the operating system to be executed in the host hardware instead in the emulated
layer. This increases performance and efficiency in execution. Unfortunately, this requires
modification in the operating system code. Some operating systems have paravirtualised distributions.

the OS or application software can use the physical hardware resources of the host computer (mainly

Cloud technology is part of our modern everyday life. In layman’s terms, we could define it as a large,
virtual and scalable compute/storage infrastructure operated by cloud service providers that can be
used on an on-demand basis for business entities or individuals for a variety of purposes. The key
advantage of the cloud is that it can free up IT users from looking after and administering hardware
and software components. The most remarkable feature of the cloud is its simplicity of use and ondemand scalability. We will look at these latter property in more detail.
For a modern enterprise relying on computers in its day-to-day operation, there are two conflicting
goals in the operation of the IT infrastructure. The first goal is to minimise the size of the infrastructure
to keep the upfront and operational cost at minimum. The second one is to have enough resources to
perform the tasks required for profitable operation. If the business can be characterised by a constant
and steady computational need (running a constant number of applications in a 24/7 fashion) the
required capacity can be planned easily. For fluctuating loads, the situation is more complicated. The
worst case is the unpredictable, burst-like load pattern.
Since companies must be able to operate even under extreme computational loads, technology
officers normally over-specify their system; they plan for the maximum possible system load. In
fluctuating or burst usage patterns, this strategy leaves the IT system underutilised most of the time
(see Figure 3-1). Underutilised periods of use represent wasted resources and operational costs. It
would be more economical to plan for the average system load level but this would leave the
infrastructure and the company vulnerable, failing to operate in the most crucial cases, i.e., during
heavy load intervals.

3.4.1 Origins of the Amazon cloud service
The Amazon company was founded in 1994 to sell books, music and electronics online. How did
Amazon ended up in the cloud world and became a leader? The story is interesting and educational.
The original concept of Amazon was to sell items online, at large scale and at very low price. Achieving
low prices required to leave out the middlemen (intermediate sales points) from the sale transactions
(i.e. to sell directly to the clients from the warehouse) and make placing and processing orders and
shipping very fast using cutting edge computerised processes. In the first few years, internal software
development was performed in a rather ad-hoc basis, without much planning for long-term operation
and growth. By the end of the nineties, the company found it more and more difficult to develop new
systems efficiently. This has become a serious issue when they were developing online shopping
technologies for Target and Marks & Spencer. It turned out that despite the experience in developing
such systems, they could not reduce the overall development time, mainly because many
infrastructure and backend components had to be created from scratch.
Recognising this problem, the company introduced an internal rule around the year 2000. Every
internal development group had to adopt the service-oriented design methodology and create welldefined interfaces (APIs) for the systems they designed. Another rule related to the use of these
services. The company ruled that every group must use service developed by the other groups inside
the company, build upon those components, if it was possible. New services could only be developed
it they were not yet available. Within few years, this has change the company a service company. The
service-oriented view appeared in their infrastructure layer as well, using virtual machines to make
their servers flexible and easy to manage. The first result of this effort was the publication of the
SOAP/XML API in 2002 that enabled programmatic access to Amazon’s shop catalogue for their
partners. In 2003, the management identified their IT hosting expertise and service-components as a
key strength and decided to create a branch in the company called Amazon Web Services (AWS) that
offered resources and services for third-party clients.

3.5 Pricing
Remember the utility computing model and Sun’s container that use a flat 1$/CPU-hour price? This
pricing scheme is called the fixed-priced on-demand or pay-as-you-go pricing. Amazon used this fixedprice scheme for its services in 2006 charged by the hours. Charge by the hour means that even if 5
minutes is used for a resource, the full hour fee has to be paid by the user. Several years later this
scheme improved, first charge unit reduced to 5 minutes, one minute, then to one second. Minute
and second based charges were especially useful for burst loads with short but heavy computational
demand. The fixed price scheme, in general, is favoured by businesses, as it makes planning, budgeting
and accounting resource use at the company very straightforward.
Further developments in the pricing strategies include the appearance of discount and dynamic
pricing. Reserved pricing requires the user to pay an up-front reservation fee for a certain amount of
resources which then will be available for at a lower, discounted rate. This is quite similar in concept
to the resource brokering mechanism in Grid computing systems where user had to reserve resources
for simultaneous execution on different computers. Another pricing alternative is spot pricing, which
uses a dynamic, auction-based pricing mechanism. Spot pricing is typically used for idle resources and
the low starting price is used to attract users to the otherwise idle resources. There are several subtle
variants of these three basic pricing schemes. We will look at some of them in the detailed overview
of a selection of cloud services.

4.1.2 Storage Services
Amazon provides three major types of cloud storage options, namely the Object, File, and Block
Storage services. The difference among the three are in the intended use and data access interface.
Amazon Simple Storage Service S3
Data in the S3 storage service is stored as objects within resources called “buckets”. A single object
can be up to 5 terabytes in size. The service can scale storage resources up and down to meet
fluctuating demands automatically. Data durability on S3 is designed for 99.999999999%. To achieve
this, S3 automatically creates and stores copies of all S3 objects across multiple systems.
S3 can also append metadata tags to objects, manage data access controls, secure data against
unauthorized users, run big data analytics, and monitor data at the object, bucket levels. Objects can
be accessed through S3 Access Points or directly through the bucket hostname. The storage service
uses different storage classes that differ in data access time, redundancy and storage size. These S3
Storage Classes are as follows: S3 Standard, S3 Intelligent-Tiering, S3 Standard-Infrequent Access, S3
One Zone-Infrequent Access, Amazon S3 Glacier, Amazon S3 Glacier Deep Archive, and S3 Outposts.
An interesting feature of S3 that it automatically moves data objects across storage classes based on

Data Security
One of the biggest fear when storing data in the cloud is security. How safe is our data? There are
users who could greatly benefit from using the cloud but refuse to do so because they feel their data
is not safe. Naturally, there are situations, sometimes due to legal regulations that prohibit data
storage outside a business entity or institute. You can think of financial and health care institutions as
good examples. In all other cases, the fear of data loss is largely unjustified.
By default, data is always encrypted in the Cloud. It is encrypted when in transit and it is stored
encrypted on storage devices. Amazon S3 supports both server-side encryption (with three key
management options) and client-side encryption for data uploads. Even if data is accidentally leaked,
it will be encrypted that makes it practically unusable for attackers. In most know cloud data leak
incidents, the cause of the data leak was the result of human error. Cloud vendors, such as Amazon,
Microsoft, Google employ the best security experts in the world and operate at a much more strict
security level than it is found in on-premises IT infrastructures. Besides encryption, Amazon S3 also
offers flexible security features to block unauthorized users from accessing your data. Any data is only
accessible by default to the user who created it. Sharing is only possible if set explicitly and controlled
through AWS access points under the supervision of the Identity and Access Management (IAM)
system. In cases when further isolation is required, virtual private clouds (VPC) can be created. Amazon
Virtual Private Cloud (Amazon VPC).

5.1 GCP Services in the IaaS Layer
Infrastructure services include compute, storage and network components. The fundamental service
in the IaaS layer is the Compute Engine that represents a Virtual Machine instance running on a
particular type of hardware/OS platform. As found at other cloud providers, the underlying GCP
hardware are grouped by their intended use. In the case of GCP, these groups represent General
Purpose (E2, N1, N2, N2D), Compute (C2), Memory (M2) and Accelerator (A2) optimised machines.
General purpose machines are meant to be used for web server and database applications. They
provide a good balance of price and performance. For high-performance and gaming applications,
engineering design, Compute optimized machines should be used. Memory optimized machines offer
large sets of memory and are ideal for data-analytic applications and for in-memory databases. If we
want to run artificial intelligence applications required GPUs or have massively parallel
implementations for HPC tasks, we can use the Accelerator optimized machines that offer different
types of NVIDA GPUs.
45

The number of cores in these virtual CPUs can range from 1 to 96 cores. Memory can be increased up
to 1.4 TB per VM. The configuration of the VM obviously affects the price. GCP Compute Engine
instances are charged by the seconds at a flat rate depending on the number of cores and RAM size
configured. There are several ways of reducing the price. GCP operates an automatic sustained use
discount. If GCP detects that our application is running a considerable time each month, it will
automatically apply a discount. If our application has a steady and predictable usage pattern, it is
possible to use a committed use discount. This can result an up to 50% discount. Finally, if our program
can run on an interruptible VM, the pre-emptible VMs are a good choice offering up to 80% discount.
There are further interesting features of the GCP Compute Engine. If required, VMs can be run on soletenant nodes, which means that a physical server node will only run our Compute Engine instances.
The node will not be shared with VM instances of other users. This is equivalent to having a dedicated
machine in our office running our applications but located in the cloud. Another form of security
guarantee is the option to use Confidential VMs. These VMs are using Confidential Computing, a new
technology that allows user data to be encrypted during computation. Traditional execution in the
cloud is based on data encrypted in storage (encryption at rest) and during data transfer (encryption
in transit) but decrypted when loaded to memory and manipulated. With Confidential Computing, it

7.2 Data processing software frameworks

7.2.1 Batch and stream data processing
The traditional way of processing data is the following. Input data is stored in multiple input files of in
a database system. A program is launched to process the input data and generate results. The program
will access and read the entire data set into memory, perform the required computation, and – once
the results are available – save the results into output files or store them in a database. The key
characteristics of this execution model is that input data is finite (bounded), static and persistent; the
program has access to the complete sets of data records and execution is staged (i.e. data input –>
data processing –> data output). This style of execution is called batch processing.

There are situations when the batch execution model is not applicable or practical. In many modern
applications, data is generated in real-time. Examples include live media (video, audio, text,
messaging), IoT systems (traffic data – autonomous driving, traffic jams; manufacturing – process and
assembly data and logs), or physical and social networks (live data, various logs, security data). In these
applications, one cannot wait for the entire input data set to become available for processing. A
continuous real-time data stream will never finish! The alternative is to process small chunks or
records of data as soon as they become available. Besides the obvious advantage that our system can
now process the data, there are other benefits as well. Since only a small portion of the entire data
set is held in memory, the resource requirements of these applications are lower. If we process data
by records, the reactiveness and latency of our system will improve. Consequently, this mode of data
processing, which we call stream-oriented data processing or simply stream processing, is beneficial
to improve performance and system utilisation and can be used even when the whole data is available
for processing. Figure 7-2 illustrated the concept of stream processing in comparison to batch
processing.

7.2.2 Apache Hadoop/MapReduce
The Hadoop/MapReduce framework is an Apache open-source project [14] created to support the
execution of large-scale batch data processing applications based on the Map-Reduce programming
55

model. The underlying infrastructure is a large cluster of commodity servers. On top of this runs
Hadoop, a software system written in Java providing a fault-tolerant and platform independent
execution environment. The system has the following main components:





HDFS - the Hadoop Distributed File System
YARN - a cluster coordinator/scheduler
MapReduce – the batch processing engine
Hadoop common – a set of common utilities

The philosophy of Hadoop/MapReduce is that large data processing tasks are executed in parallel. The
input data is divided into smaller chunks that are assigned to different servers for simultaneous
execution (the “Map” phase), than the partial results are collected to form the final results (“Reduce”
phase). The input data is stored in a large fault-tolerant distributed filesystem. Hadoop is built on the
assumption that in a distributed system failure is not the exception but the norm. If we use thousands
of servers, there will always be one that is not working; therefore, fault-tolerance has to be an
essential feature of the runtime system. Hadoop provides application-level fault tolerance support.
The system consist of compute and storage nodes; these are normally are the same, which allows job
execution without unnecessary extra data transfer. Jobs are submitted to the system from clients,
then the Hadoop execution environment takes over the scheduling and management of the jobs.
Execution is controlled by the YARN engine that consists of a single master ResourceManager, one
worker NodeManager per cluster-node, and MRAppMaster per application (see Figure 7-3). The job
(JAR file or executable) will be received by the ResourceManager, which schedules the subtasks to the
nodes and provide monitoring and feedback to clients using information received from the
NodeManagers.
A MapReduce application has a simple structure that can be followed with a programming model
provided by interfaces. The execution model is as follows:
1.
2.
3.
4.
5.

read data set from filesystem
divide data into chunks (key-value pairs)
apply computation to subsets on different nodes: Map process
reduce value of each key: Reduce process
write result back to filesystem

In general, a MapReduce program will create a set of key-value pairs while executing the various
stages of the program, as shown here
(input) <k1,v1> -> map -> <k2,v2> -> combine -> <k2,v2> -> reduce -> <k3,v3> (output)

K1, K2, K3 and V1, V2, V3 are different key and value classes, which must all be serializable for
execution. The execution of the program involves several phases, such as





Input phase: the input phase reads and parses input data and forwards it to the Mapper in the
form of key-value pairs
Map phase: user-defined processing implemented as a function that processes incoming keyvalue pairs and generate new output key-value pairs. Intermediate keys and values are used to
forward the results of the Mapper to the next phase.
Combiner phase: this is an optional step used to do local reduce operations on a single node
(Mapper process).

56






Shuffle and Sort phase: this is the starting step of the Reducer process. The Reducer will sort the
input key-value pairs by the key values to make the reduction process more efficient.
Reducer phase: in this phase, the user-defined reduction function is executed to aggregate, filter
or combine the data produced by the map processes. The Reducer will output zero more keyvalue pairs as the result of the program.
Output phase: the final phase of the program which takes the result key-value pairs and saves
them in the required format to the file system.

import java.util.StringTokenizer;
import org.apache.hadoop.conf.Configuration;
import org.apache.hadoop.fs.Path;
import org.apache.hadoop.io.IntWritable;
import org.apache.hadoop.io.Text;
import org.apache.hadoop.mapreduce.Job;
import org.apache.hadoop.mapreduce.Mapper;
import org.apache.hadoop.mapreduce.Reducer;
import org.apache.hadoop.mapreduce.lib.input.FileInputFormat;
import org.apache.hadoop.mapreduce.lib.output.FileOutputFormat;
public class WordCount {
public static class TokenizerMapper extends Mapper<Object, Text, Text, IntWritable>{
private final static IntWritable one = new IntWritable(1);
private Text word = new Text();
public void map(Object key, Text value, Context context) throws IOException,
InterruptedException {
StringTokenizer itr = new StringTokenizer(value.toString());
while (itr.hasMoreTokens()) {
word.set(itr.nextToken());
context.write(word, one);
}
}
}
public static class IntSumReducer extends Reducer<Text,IntWritable,Text,IntWritable> {
private IntWritable result = new IntWritable();
public void reduce(Text key, Iterable<IntWritable> values, Context context) throws
IOException, InterruptedException {
int sum = 0;
for (IntWritable val : values) {
sum += val.get();
}
result.set(sum);
context.write(key, result);
}
}

7.2.3 Apache Spark
The Hadoop/MapReduce framework is a widely used system to process extremely large datasets. It
has one fundamental limitations though, which is that it uses the file system for intermediate data
transfer. This is serious performance bottleneck, since file system bandwidth is several orders of
magnitudes lower than memory bandwidth. If a system is used for processing large datasets, this may
become a prohibitive problem. Identifying this deficiency, a MapReduce side project was started in
2009 to create an in-memory version of MapReduce. Few years later, this project became an
independent Apache project named Spark [15].
Spark is a very fast alternative framework executing MapReduce style programs. When using inmemory operations, it can be up to 100 times faster than a Hadoop/MapReduce program. If it is
running on disks, it can still be up to 10 times faster due to the reduced number of I/O operations
required. Besides speed, Spark has other advantages. It provides APIs for several languages, e.g. , and
provides richer functionalities than the ‘Map’ and ‘Reduce’ steps: it supports SQL queries, machine
learning and graph analytics operations as well as streaming data processing capabilities.
Spark has its own built-in cluster management system, which means it can be executed without the
help of other cluster execution environments. However, if required, it can make use of third party
cluster runtimes. The execution modes of Spark are as follows:




Standalone mode: Spark will run as an independent cluster-based environment.
Hadoop mode: Spark will run on top of the Hadoop YARN cluster management system.
Spark in MapReduce mode: Spark jobs will be executed as Hadoop/MapReduce jobs. This mode
helps in integrating legacy MapReduce programs with Spark.

Spark has become the leading data processing/analytics framework. Its rich functionality and high
performance made it successful in a large variety in application areas. Figure 7-5 illustrates the Spark
layered architecture as well as its support to various application programming technologies, execution

8 Data stream processing
As discussed in Section 7.2.1, not all data processing tasks can be performed in the batch execution
style. Continuous data streams, e.g. real-time measurement or monitoring data, cannot be processed
with the traditional input—process—output paradigm. In these cases, streaming data processing and
stream algorithms can help us.
In addition to providing an execution mechanism, stream processing can improve system performance
(decrease latency) and reduce resource consumption (less memory is used as only a portion of data is
used at any one time during processing).
In this section, we briefly overview the key characteristics of stream processing, then overview and
compare the most popular stream data processing software frameworks. We will finish this chapter
with an introduction to the Beam framework and its use in the Google Cloud Platform.

The importance of Welford’s algorithm is that besides providing a numerically stable one-pass method
for calculating the mean and variance, it can be used without having the entire data set known. It can
be used for a data sequence of unknown length. It also demonstrates that streaming algorithms often
require algorithmic changes sometimes going back to the fundamental mathematical basis to find a
solution that can be executed in a streaming version. The calculation of variance is a nice example of
this.
Assume a producer-consumer system, where the producer generates a series of data elements and
sends them to the consumer that will calculate the mean and variance of the data. The Welford
algorithm can be used for the calculation of this data stream without the need to store the data
elements in memory. The Java implementation of the consumer is below. We further assume that the
producer and consumer communicate via an ArrayBlockingQueue instance.
private class Consumer extends Thread{
private ArrayBlockingQueue data;
private long n;
private double mean;
private double sn;
private double variance;
public Consumer(ArrayBlockingQueue data) {
this.data = data;
}
public void run(){
while (true) {
try {
int x = data.take();
n++;
double oldMean = mean;
mean = mean + (x - mean)/n;
sn = sn + (x - mean)* (x - oldMean);
variance = sn/n;
} catch (InterruptedException ex) {
ex.printStacktrace();
}
}
}
}

8.2 Popular stream data processing frameworks
The 24/7 style availability and operation of applications in the cloud brought with it the emergence of
data stream applications. If we can compute continuously in the cloud, why not use this capability for
processing continuous data streams. The proliferation of these applications then, naturally, led to the

8.2.2 Apache Flink
The most disturbing feature of streaming data processing is that requires new implementations for
our well-known traditional batch algorithms. In other words, it is a disruptive technology. If one wants
to do live data processing, new implementations (meaning program development effort) are required.
This means that developers must develop and maintain two codebases, one for batch and one for
stream processing which is very undesirable.
This is where Apache Flink [19] comes into play. The developers of Flink set out the goal of unifying
the stream and batch programming models into one common programming model. The key concept
of Flink is the data stream within which we distinguish between unbounded and bounded streams
(see Figure 8-4). Unbounded streams that only have a start but no end represent live streaming data,
whereas bounded streams (with well-defined start and end) are used to describe batch-processed
data. Treating traditional batch input data as a bounded stream removes the need for keeping
duplicate implementations. Now the same algorithm can work on both types of streams.

Figure 8-4 The unified treatment of bounded and unbounded streams in Flink
Flink is a feature-rich stream processing engine. As illustrated in Figure 8-5, it can receive data from a
variety of sources, can interact with different execution and storage systems and forward results to a
number of different data consumers (file system, databases, applications, event queues).

Figure 8-5 Rich integration features provide fast development in Flink16
Handling state
One of the key problems of large distributed systems is state. In the presence of failures and errors, it
is very difficult to maintain a consistent application state. As a result, many large distributed systems
favour or explicitly demand stateless design.

and the implementation is very efficient as it is based on in-memory state representation (see Figure

Time is another important feature of streaming applications. In real-life scenarios, events have
causality; one event cannot happen before the other, e.g. we cannot hear the sound of a gun fire
before the gun has been triggered. Consequently, stream data most frequently include time stamps
that can be used to guarantee event causality. As a result, stream oriented systems can distinguish
processing based on event or processing time. In event-time mode, applications will process events
based on their timestamp, guaranteeing consistent results independent of how long the actual
processing lasts. On the other hand, in processing-time mode, applications process events based on
processing time, e.g. in real-time applications with defined latency requirements. In this case, only
events received up to the processing deadline will be considered, meaning that some events may be
missed. Flink supports both time modes. Applications can use either event or processing time mode
semantics. In event-time mode, further subtleties, e.g. watermark support and late event handling
can help in achieving the required processing semantics.
Most event streams have inherent time semantics because each event is produced at a specific point
in time. Moreover, many common stream computations are based on time, such as windows
aggregations, sessionization, pattern detection, and time-based joins. An important aspect of stream
processing is how an application measures time, i.e., the difference of event-time and processingtime. The details of these can be found in the detailed Flink documentation.

8.2.3 Apache Storm / Trident
Apache Storm [20] is an alternative take on the MapReduce/Stream processing problem. Storm
approaches this from the unbound stream processing architecture point of view. Imaging an
application that runs continuously until it is stopped. The incoming data is processed continuously by
a generalised producer-consumer system as a dataflow engine. The goal of Storm is to create an
abstraction for this engine and simplify the development of applications fitting this execution strategy.
Creating a Producer-Consumer implementation in Java is relatively simple. However, to create one
that has multiple data paths in it, fault-tolerant and scalable (new nodes can be added and remove at
will without the need to restart the application) is an extremely complicated task.
The underlying concept of Storm is the Topology that represents the above generalised producerconsumer dataflow graph. The topology processes incoming data streams, unbounded data that is
continuously arriving at the system. Sources of data streams at the edge of the topology are
73

represented by special objects called Spouts. Data sources can be APIs, queues, etc. that produce data
to be operated on. The nodes of the topology are Bolts that represent a processing step. A Bolt can
consume streams, apply an operation to them, and output the result as a stream. As shown in Figure
8-7, Bolts are connected to each of the spouts, and then connect to each other to arrange all of the
necessary processing. The connecting edges are communication links on which stream data elements
(tuples) are transferred. At the end of the topology, a final bolt output may be used as an input for a
connected system.

Figure 8-7 A Storm topology consisting of Spouts and Bolts.

A Storm topology is executed on top of a distributed cluster architecture. As show in Figure 8-8, the
entry point to the cluster is the Nimbus component. This works similar to the submission point of

public static class WordCount extends BaseBasicBolt {

count++;
counts.put(word, count);
collector.emit(new Values(word, count));
}

8.2.4 Apache Kafka
Apache Kafka [20] is a high-performance message broker architecture designed to build real-time
streaming data pipelines. It is commonly used as data delivery system in various streaming
applications. Although Kafka can be used for stream processing in itself, its main use is typically is
streaming data delivery only.
Kafka streams consists of records. Records belong to categories that in Kafka are called topics. Each
record consists of a key, a value, and a timestamp.
In contrast to traditional point-to-point message delivery systems, Kafka is a publish-subscribe
architecture. It uses multiple message queues internally for topics and subscribers can subscribe for
messages arriving to a given topic. The system guarantees that all subscribers will receive the event
of interest. Kafka is a reliable, fault-tolerant system. It uses a distributed cluster with automatic
partitioning and replication guaranteeing zero message loss even in presence of hardware failures. It
is also very fast, and integrates nicely with the Apache Spark and Storm systems.
The high-level view of Kafka and its place in a streaming application is illustrated in Figure 8-10.
Multiple producers can feed records into the Kafka system that are delivered to multiple consumers.
In addition, Kafka can be connected to databased and stream processors to perform data processing.

77

Figure 8-10 The place of Kafka within a stream processing application.
A Kafka cluster consists of several Kafka Brokers. A Broker is basically a Kafka cluster node. Brokers
manage producers and consumers, store messages and provide fault tolerance by replication. To
support load balancing and reliability, each topic is stored in a broker in form of several message
partitions. A partition is a queue in which the system collects messages. A partition is show in Figure
8-11, having one producer writing to the tail of the queue and multiple consumers reading messages.
Message positions are marked by offset values.

Figure 8-11 Implementation of a topic partition as a queue
with interacting producers and consumers
Figure 8-12 shows the internals of a Kafka cluster consisting of two brokers. The cluster has only one
topic, Topic A. The topic is split into two partitions, partition 0 and 1. In a cluster, one node is a leader
while the others are the followers. The role of the followers is to replicate the data stored by the
leader and in case of a leader failure, to take over the leader role. In this particular case, Broker 1 is
the leader for Topic A Partition 0 (A/0 in short), which means that Broker 2 will receive a replica of
A/0. Broker 2, however, is the leader for Topic A Partition 1 (A/1) so Broker 1 will receive a replica of
A/1. Leader election and partition replication is performed automatically behind the scenes. When
producers submit messages to the cluster, the system will automatically route the message to the
corresponding leader (see Messages for A/0 and A/1); similarly, consumers will receive messages from
the leader of the partition the message originates from. We do not need to understand the more
intricate details of message persistence, acknowledgements, replications, etc. This was describe only
to make us appreciate the complexity that lies in implementing a fault-tolerant, reliable and scalable
messaging system.

8.2.5 Apache Beam/GCP Dataflow
The last streaming framework we discuss is Apache Beam [21], which provides a unified programming
abstraction for batched and streaming data processing. It is an open-source framework, just as the
previous Apache systems but its importance lies in the fact that it is the programming system used by
Google Dataflow, the GCP stream processing engine service. That is, if you want to run large streaming
application on the Google Cloud Platform, Beam is the natural choice as it is tightly integrated with
Dataflow as its back-end. Additional benefits of using Dataflow come from the fact that it is nicely
integrated with other Google Services, as shown in Figure 8-13, making it a very powerful application
development and execution platform.

80

Figure 8-13 Integration of Google Dataflow and the Beam SDK in the GCP service architecture.
We will start with an overview the Beam system and its programming model. Beam uses the concept
of the pipeline to describe the program. The pipeline represents a program from its start to finish,
responsible for data input, processing and data output. Once a pipeline is created, it can be executed
on a back-end which is called a runner. The Beam pipeline is presented in a Java program as an instance
of the class Pipeline. The next item is the class PCollection, which represents a distributed data
set used in the execution of the Beam program. A PCollection data can be bounded or unbounded,
depending on the input used. The third item in the model is the class PTransform that represents one
step of operation executed in the program. Transforms can change, filter, group, analyse, or otherwise
process the elements in a PCollection. A transform creates a new output PCollection without
modifying the input collection. A transform can have one or more PCollections as its input and can
produce zero or more PCollection objects as its output. A simple sample pipeline is shown in Figure
8-14 consisting of one input data source, three transforms.

Figure 8-14 A simple Beam pipeline using three transforms

This pipeline can be described in the Beam Java programming model by the following code skeleton.
[Final Output PCollection] =
[Initial Input PCollection]
.apply([First Transform])
.apply([Second Transform])
.apply([Third Transform]);

81

The initial PCollection object produced by the database read operation will be used to execute the
“first transform”. This returns an intermediate PCollection – the output of the first transform – that
can be used to invoke the second transform. The next returned object is used for executing the third
transform, which results in the last PCollection that can be persisted to database. Reading and writing
data to and from the pipeline requires IO transforms. Beam provides a variety of specialised IO
transforms that perform input/output operations with the external world. Note the apply() method
of the PCollection class; this method is responsible for executing the transform at the specified
position of the pipeline.
Several core transform are already implemented in Beam, such as ParDo, GroupByKey, CoGroupByKey,
Combine, Flatten and Partition. ParDo transforms each element of a PCollection in parallel; it can
be used for transforming elements, outputting them to a new PCollection or performing calculation
on the elements. The description of the other transforms can be found in the Beam documentation.
Once the pipeline is constructed, a driver program is required that executes the pipeline on the
selected backend runner. The role of the driver program is to configure the backend runner, create
the pipeline and run the program.

Step 1: A root transform, the TextIO.Read will read a set of input text files. TextIO.Read returns a

a PCollection to a series of text files.

8.3 Comparison of stream frameworks
In the previous section, we overviewed quite a number of data stream processing frameworks without
the goal of completeness. We have excluded several other frameworks for reasons of brevity. The
question then naturally arises, if there are so many streaming frameworks, how can we choose which
on is the best or which one we should choose to solve a particular problem. Is there a way to compare
them objectively?
In this section, we provide guidelines to how these frameworks should be compared, what features
or characteristics should be examined when selecting a system as a basis for implementations.
Since the sole reason of using software frameworks is to reduce the development time by relying on
existing implementations, the first set of categories we explore is related to the framework as a tool
for developers, such as the complexity, maintainability, friendliness and maturity of the framework.
Framework complexity: We need to examine how difficult it is the set up and monitor the system,
what level of support it provides for these tasks; how complex is the set of dependencies; are there
version limitations, backward compatibility issues, etc.
Framework maintainability: Here we are interested in whether the framework is vendor specific or
cloud provider agnostic; it can be used with arbitrary container technologies, if required; and whether
it has sufficient and high quality system documentation.
Framework friendliness: It is a key property of a framework how easy it is to understand the model
of operation and the programming abstraction. In addition, how many parameters are to be tuned to
get the system working in an optimal way. The richness and cleanness of the API is also of high
83

importance. Finally, developers like frameworks that can be used from/with several programming
languages.
Framework maturity: Since choosing a framework is a major decision determining future
developments for a long time, it is very important that a system has strong support. Since we have
only discussed open-source frameworks, this typically means community support. Hence, the size and
vibrancy of the user community are very important indicators. The number of contributors, commit
activity, time to fix bugs and frequency of releases, and the number of companies adopting the
framework are also good indicators of a successful system.
Moving on to more technical issues, we can compare properties such as the type of streaming
supported in the system, message delivery guarantees, latency and throughput, resource
consumption, programming model. Several publications can be found comparing streaming
frameworks in great depth [22]–[26], here we only highlight the most important characteristics.
Type of streaming can be native or microbatch based. Native streaming means processing every
received individual data record. Microbatch, on the other hand collects several records into a small
batch that is then processed in one operation. Native streaming naturally provides lower latencies but
can have a negative effect on throughput in the presence of message checkpointing. Microbatch
operation comes with higher latencies but can give improved throughput due to lower message
handling overheads.
One of the most important properties of any streaming system is reliability and fault-tolerance. This
is normally expressed in the form of message delivery guaranties. There are three message delivery
semantics in any distributed system: At-Most-Once, At-Least-Once and Exactly Once semantics. AtMost Once semantics means that any message is attempted to be deliver only once. If message
delivery fails for any reason, there will be no attempts for repeat sending of that message. In short, in
such a system, messages can be lost. At-Least-Once semantics provides guarantees that any message
will be received by the consumer but some messages may be received several times due to repeat
send operation on error conditions. In these systems, the application must keep track of the messages
and handle the unnecessary duplicate messages. The Exactly Once semantics is the strongest form of
guarantee; this ensures that every message is delivered to the consumer and only once. The
application does not have to worry about duplicate messages. Normally, message delivery semantics
is coupled with message order guarantees. With At-Most-Once and At-Least-Once semantics, the
order of messages in delivery may not be guaranteed to follow sending order. Exactly Once semantics
provides exact message ordering as well.
A further technical issue is the type of programming abstraction used for expressing stream processing
in the framework. One programming style is compositional, where programmers must build the
processing topology by hand from building blocks (data sources, operators, transforms, data sinks,
etc.) and components are defined by interfaces. The other style is declarative, where the processing
pipeline is created automatically behind the scene, the programmer only has to use/provide operators
as functions. This mode results in a functional programming style and can rely on lambda expressions
heavily.
Since a full in-depth comparison of all known streaming frameworks is beyond the scope of this book,
we finish this section with a small illustrative example that highlights how these systems can be
compared. The feature table is shown in Figure 8-15. The Beam system is not part of this comparison.

Cloud Functions represent the latest development in cloud execution technology. The key feature of

synchronous RPC-style calls is not a suitable option for large distributed applications; loosely coupled
communication based on messages is the only viable strategy for large, scalable applications.
Consequently, all cloud vendors provide some sort of messaging service in their product list that help
plugging components together. In this section, we will focus on the messaging service provided in the
Google Cloud Platform, the Google Pub/Sub service.

10.1 Google Pub/Sub overview
The Google Pub/Sub is a high-availability, asynchronous, real-time messaging service based on the
publish-subscribe philosophy. Pub/Sub is a low-latency, high-throughput system that operates in all
Google Cloud regions providing a universal messaging and event delivery infrastructure for global
cloud applications. Pub/Sub can be used as a general messaging middleware or event delivery system
triggering Cloud Functions or as a data streaming layer.
Pub/Sub connects Publishers and Subscribers through Topics. A Topic is a named resource to which
publishers send messages. Subscribers create Subscriptions that are named resources representing
the stream of messages from a single, specific topic, to be delivered to the subscribing application. A
Message is a combination of data and (optional) attributes that a publisher sends to a topic and is
eventually delivered to subscribers. The message attribute is a key-value pair that a publisher can
define for a message.
The actual Pub/Sub Message format is as follows. A message consists of fields with the message data
and metadata. At least one of the following fields must be present in the message:




The message data
An ordering key
Attributes with additional metadata

The Pub/Sub service adds the following fields to the message:



A message ID unique to the topic
A timestamp for when the Pub/Sub service receives the message

Pub/Sub is an integral part of the Google Cloud infrastructure and as such is supported by all Google
Services. This makes integration of GCP services an almost trivial task (see Figure 10-1 for a set of key
services that can be used with Pub/Sub).
Pub/Sub can connect very different types of components into a unified cloud application. Figure 10-2
illustrates this in more detail. Any application that can make HTTPS requests to
pubsub.googleapis.com can be a Publisher, e.g. an App Engine app, a web service hosted on Google
Compute Engine or any other third-party network, or an app installed on a desktop or mobile device.
Similarly, any application that can make HTTPS requests to pubsub.googleapis.com can be a Pull
Subscriber.

arrives from Publisher C, the same message is delivered via subscription YC to Subscriber Y and via
subscription ZC to Subscriber Z.

10.2 Pub/Sub message flow
In this section, we examine the steps of message delivery in details. The delivery of message sent from
a Publisher involves the following steps in the Pub/Sub infrastructure.
1. A publisher application creates a topic in the Pub/Sub service and sends messages to the topic.
A message contains a payload and optional attributes that describe the payload content.
2. The service ensures that published messages are retained on behalf of subscriptions. A
published message is retained for a subscription until it is acknowledged by any subscriber
consuming messages from that subscription.
3. Pub/Sub forwards messages from a topic to all of its subscriptions, individually.
4. A subscriber receives messages either by Pub/Sub pushing them to the subscriber's chosen
endpoint, or by the subscriber pulling them from the service.
5. The subscriber sends an acknowledgement to the Pub/Sub service for each received message.
6. The service removes acknowledged messages from the subscription's message queue.
93

Pub/Sub, by default, delivers messages with At-Least-Once semantics. The only exception to message
delivery is if a message cannot be delivered within 7 days after its generation, in which case it is
deleted after 7 days without delivery. Similarly, if a message does not have a subscription at the time
of its generation, that message will not be delivered at all.
When Pub/Sub is used with the Beam SDK streaming programming model, it is possible to achieve
Exactly-Once semantics with ordered message delivery.

AnnotateImageRequest.newBuilder().addFeatures(textFeature).setImage(img).build();
visionRequests.add(visionRequest);
// Detect text in an image using the Cloud Vision API
AnnotateImageResponse visionResponse;
try (ImageAnnotatorClient client = ImageAnnotatorClient.create()) {
visionResponse = client.batchAnnotateImages(visionRequests).getResponses(0);
if (visionResponse == null || !visionResponse.hasFullTextAnnotation()) {
logger.info(String.format("Image %s contains no text", filename));
return;
}
if (visionResponse.hasError()) {
// Log error

106

logger.log(Level.SEVERE, "Error in vision API call: " +
visionResponse.getError().getMessage());
return;
}
} catch (IOException e) {
// Log error (since IOException cannot be thrown by a Cloud Function)
logger.log(Level.SEVERE, "Error detecting text: " + e.getMessage(), e);
return;
}
String text = visionResponse.getFullTextAnnotation().getText();
logger.info("Extracted text from image: " + text);
// Detect language using the Cloud Translation API
DetectLanguageRequest languageRequest =
DetectLanguageRequest.newBuilder()
.setParent(LOCATION_NAME)
.setMimeType("text/plain")
.setContent(text)
.build();
DetectLanguageResponse languageResponse;
try (TranslationServiceClient client = TranslationServiceClient.create()) {
languageResponse = client.detectLanguage(languageRequest);
} catch (IOException e) {
// Log error (since IOException cannot be thrown by a function)
logger.log(Level.SEVERE, "Error detecting language: " + e.getMessage(), e);
return;
}
if (languageResponse.getLanguagesCount() == 0) {
logger.info("No languages were detected for text: " + text);
return;
}
String languageCode = languageResponse.getLanguages(0).getLanguageCode();
logger.info(String.format("Detected language %s for file %s", languageCode, filename));
// Send a Pub/Sub translation request for every language we're going to translate to
for (String targetLanguage : TO_LANGS) {
logger.info("Sending translation request for language " + targetLanguage);
OcrTranslateApiMessage message = new OcrTranslateApiMessage(
text, filename, targetLanguage);
ByteString byteStr = ByteString.copyFrom(message.toPubsubData());
PubsubMessage pubsubApiMessage = PubsubMessage.newBuilder().setData(byteStr).build();
try {
publisher.publish(pubsubApiMessage).get();
} catch (InterruptedException | ExecutionException e) {
// Log error
logger.log(Level.SEVERE, "Error publishing translation request: " + e.getMessage(),
e);
return;
}
}
}

The next step of the application is to actually perform the translations. The class OcrTranslateText
below implements a background function that responds to translation request messages. The accept
method is invoked for each received message. As the first step of processing, the method extracts the
source text and the target language code from the incoming message, then creates an instance of the
TranslationServiceClient class. An appropriate translation request object is created then the text
is translated by invoking the translateText method. The returned translated text and the name of

107

the output file is packaged into a new Pub/Sub message and sent to the cloud using the publish
method on the publisher created in the constructor or the class.

To complete the application, the above functions must be deployed in the cloud, followed by the
creation of the input and output Storage bucket as well as Pub/Sub topics. Once these steps are
complete, an upload of an image file to the input bucket will trigger the execution of the application.
This sample illustrated the key concepts of cloud service orchestration as well as the simplicity and
elegance of the use of loosely coupled message/event communication among services. The developer
does not have to deal with infrastructure management details, can concentrate on the application
logic only. Thanks to the automatic scaling property of Cloud Functions, the application will be able to
service arbitrary number of clients without any further intervention.
The interested reader should consult the Google Cloud documentation for further samples on service
orchestration.

13 Conclusions
The goal of this book was to provide background material for the Cloud Programming MSc course at
the University of Pannonia. As such, it was not meant to be a definitive work covering all theoretical
and practical aspects of cloud computing. The focus of this course is on programming cloud systems
in contrast to creating and managing cloud infrastructures, hence system management topics were
largely ignored. It was also not meant to provide full coverage on programming at the level of detail
that would be required for professional cloud developers. Nonetheless, I hope it serves as a useful
introduction for the key concepts and provides enough practical programming details to get interest
students started in the very exciting cloud computing world.
We know that the future can never be predicted accurately but we can say with a high level of
confidence that the role and capability of cloud systems will continually expand in the coming years.
This will have important consequences for developers and the entire software industry. As web
technology had a major impact on desktop and mobile applications, we can expect that cloud will have
a similarly significant effect on future software. It is expected from applications already to integrate
with the cloud at least for input/output operations so we can process our data stored in the cloud or
save our results in the cloud. Future applications will rely on cloud services more and more, leading to
true cloud-based applications built from cloud service components.
Cloud vendors will do their best to improve further the availability, reliability and security of their
systems, and the increasing competition and the use of clean energy are expected to reduce prices in
the long term, making cloud systems and cloud computing an affordable and trusted platform for
mankind. There are several trends that make future cloud computing even more interesting that it is
today. High-performance computing and artificial intelligence are finding their way into cloud systems.
It is expected that cloud vendors will soon provide on-demand supercomputing resources and access
to infrastructure designed specifically for AI workloads. This will provide a platform for innovation and
possibly will lead to the development of new services, even industries. Internet-of-Things based data
analytics is expected to revolutionise manufacturing and logistics. When used with body sensors
coupled with cloud-based health-care and diagnostics, cloud computing can revolutionise our healthcare system as well.
These were just a few examples or glimpses of what the future could bring in cloud computing, but
we can say that this future is certainly bright and exciting. So, do not get left behind then, stay tuned
and become part of the future!
